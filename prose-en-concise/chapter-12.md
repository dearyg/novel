# Chapter 12 — 0x0C: The Three-Body Problem

They walked because Kael would not get in the car.

"It's two miles for me every day. You can do it once." Long flat stride — the pace of a man who had learned to cover ground without spending anything he could not afford.

It was the same walk Jeff had taken in {{user_location}} the day he found the billboard. He had walked it as an intruder. He walked it now beside the man who lived there, and the only thing that had changed was the company, and the company had changed everything.

"Twelve years. Pacific Routing. The AI came for the juniors first — that's the part nobody gets right. By 2026 three of us left in a department of forty. Friday in June: *the system has flagged your position as redundant.*" He said the word the way you'd hold up something pulled from a drain. "I'd spent twelve years being the redundancy. The day they didn't need redundancy, I found out that's all I'd ever been."

"It's one performance review. I've run the trace. I'm one managerial decision from your notebook."

"You got the warm partition, I got the rain. Same architecture. Different allocation."

---

They passed a bus stop and Jeff stopped, because the poster stopped him.

*The Anchor: Convergence.* The Anchor's hand on a glowing orb, pushing it away. *The strongest choice is letting go.*

Jeff's right hand had gone, without instruction, to his jacket pocket, where the walnut phone sat screen-down against his hip — forty-two grams of un-networked wood, offline, un-queryable. "He had a sealed thing. The one clean signal in his whole life."

"And the marketing department wants him to throw it in a hole," Kael said.

Jeff took his thumb off the grain. "Come on. Marcus is waiting."

---

Marcus Meridian: double-wide trailer, fifty-three, gold-and-walnut pen from his father's desk in 2011, uncapped and recapped as he was about to be doubted. Ayla Reyes, Meridian Mars aerospace systems lead, was already there with a *MARS OR BUST* mug she would never drink from. Ruth had sent a paper letter: *do whatever you were going to do. I listen Thursday at seven. Love, R.*

---

"We pool our data. Merge it. Decide what it means. Not before."

Ayla looked at Kael: "Why is the logistics engineer here."

"Because I'm the only person in this room who doesn't draw a check from your brother."

Jeff: ninety-seven-event anomaly corpus, Primary Key collisions across fourteen individuals, fifteen-node complete graph. Marcus: eleven patients, seven retinal matches, four synchronized cardiac events — one at Jeff's shoulder-bruise timestamp in April. Ayla: three friendly-fire incidents, same Nash-equilibrium failure in Dr. Itzel Velarde's drone-operator framework; Julian had authorized her to share it with *any* team investigating Afterlife.

"Did he send you." / "Did not send me. He is waiting to see what we find before he decides what he is."

Kael: "A man leaving a door unlocked and going for a long walk. Carry on."

---

Kael's data: twelve years old, handwritten. Route-logistics anomalies, 2019 to 2026. "The allocator has no legal claim on an analog notebook I wrote in my own hand. The one asset I own outright." Both hands on it a second longer than needed.

*Aion* ran a cross-correlation lag-finder, anchored on a shared sneeze: one of Marcus's patients and one driver in Kael's notebook, same day, different neighborhoods, neither knowing the other existed. Marcus's clock seventy-two seconds slow. Kael's Casio from 2011 — two minutes three seconds fast.

*Forty-one anomalous overlap events. Geographic clustering non-random — seven US cities plus Lagos, Osaka, São Paulo. Correlations predate Afterlife's launch by more than a decade.*

"Not contamination spreading from one source. Fifteen lives, subtly correlated since before any of them met. The anomaly didn't begin when I started feeling it. The instruments got good enough to see a thing that was always there."

Ayla held the coffee for the first time without drinking it. "The thing is never absent before the instrument. The instrument is absent before the thing."

---

Marcus had not spoken in eleven minutes. He read from a sheet like a chart.

"My patients with biometric overlaps do not share networks. Three are Amish. One hasn't seen a screen since 2019. The simplest explanation I cannot break: the patients are partitions of one underlying state. The boundary between them is a configuration. Not a fact.

"I withheld it on purpose. Your graph had to be built by someone who had never heard my sentence. Your data does not break it. Two people who arrived at the same shape from different data, with no contact. That is replication."

"It is the difference between a man and a phenomenon."

Kael turned his cup a quarter turn. "Nietzsche. Perspectivism. Three biased instruments — a doctor who sees bodies, a logger who sees logs, me who sees routes. None of us can see the object. The angles agree. Perspectivism doesn't prove there's no truth. It proves you need more than one of you to triangulate it."

"You're saying we're an ensemble."

"You've been one for an hour. Combine independent models and the errors cancel because they don't line up. What's left is the part you all got right. Then train a fourth model whose only job is to weight the other three. Stacking beats voting every time the members are independent."

---

They played basketball at noon because the thing on the table had gotten too large for the trailer. Three neighborhood kids made room.

*Prediction,* *Aion* said in Jeff's earpiece. *Motor-sync event between you and Kael in the next ninety seconds. Logging it now so you cannot back-fit the result.*

Forty-one seconds later he drove baseline, Kael cut, Jeff passed without looking and Kael caught without looking. The kid said *that was sick.* They exchanged a look aimed a third of a meter to the left of each other, at a thing neither of them could see.

*Motor planning aligned two point three milliseconds before contact — below conscious reaction time. A thing I can predict is a thing I have modeled.*

Jeff missed the next shot on purpose, because winning felt like the wrong response to having been seen.

---

At 2:14 three Meridian security uniforms walked in with a compliance warrant. Nine patient files. Left at 3:03.

"*Aion* pushed to three locations six minutes before they arrived — my garage, Ayla's encrypted orbital backup, Ruth's container via paper courier. Byzantine fault tolerance: three nodes tolerate one corruption." Kael was the fourth — analog, unsubpoenable, the notebook never digital. "You can't image a thing that was never digital."

Outside, a bus rolled past with the Afterlife wrap — *LEAVE THE PAIN BEHIND — 30 DAYS* — the counter ticking toward the date they were now officially racing. Marcus watched it go and capped the pen and said nothing, which from Marcus was the loudest thing in the room.

---

In the parking lot Ayla held an unopened coffee.

"Julian: Geneva, the fifteenth, no contract. *I am ready to see the whole of what I built.*"

"Is he really." / "He is trying. And this is me, not him: if Afterlife is going to be stopped, it has to be stopped by Julian. The board is already past him. He owns the infrastructure. He is the last lever. You go to him, or it launches."

She drove off without waiting for an answer. Jeff stood in the lot and did not say *I'll watch her taillights* even to himself, because he was learning that the things he narrated to himself were the things he was avoiding feeling, and he had decided, somewhere in the last six weeks, to feel them instead.

Kael came out with the notebook. "How long has Ruth been — "

"Since before us. That's the answer to every question you're going to ask me for the next three days. Come on, tech guy."

---

That night Jeff slept, for the first time in six weeks, a full seven hours, on the floor of Ruth's shipping container under a wool blanket that smelled of coffee and printer's ink. He dreamed of a man he had not met, on a plane, looking down through a break in the cloud at a long white shape on the water.
