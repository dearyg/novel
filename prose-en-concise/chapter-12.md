# Chapter 12 — 0x0C: The Three-Body Problem

Marcus Meridian's clinic — a double-wide trailer three blocks west of Kael's billboard, on a lot rented from a church that had stopped having services in 2024. Isopropyl alcohol and old paperbacks. Four folding chairs and a water cooler.

Marcus, fifty-six, six-one, broad-shouldered, gray hair, gold-and-walnut fountain pen in his shirt pocket — the one thing he had taken from his father's desk after the funeral in 2011.

"Jeff. Coffee. Now."

"Yes please."

Jeff arrived with Kael at 8:47 Saturday. Ayla Reyes had been there an hour, drinking from a *MARS OR BUST* mug. Five-ten, wiry, the economical physicality of people who had spent too many years in centrifuge training.

Ruth had sent a paper letter, taped to the equipment-room door: *Gentlemen, Ayla, do whatever you were going to do. Publish or don't. I listen Thursday seven PM. Love, R.*

---

"All right. We pool our data."

Ayla looked at Marcus. "Why is the logistics engineer here."

Kael, without looking up: "Because I'm the only person in this room who doesn't work for your brother."

"Fair."

Jeff: "Three things. An anomaly corpus with ninety-seven events. Primary Key collision records across fourteen individuals. The fifteen-node complete graph."

Marcus: "Eleven clinical patients over eighteen months, none genetically related. Seven retinal matches. Three dental-metric matches. Four synchronized cardiac events, including one at the timestamp Jeff's shoulder bruised in April."

Ayla: "Meridian Aerospace internal memos. Dr. Itzel Velarde's framework trained both Bolivian and Peruvian drone-operator tactics with the same model backbone. Three friendly-fire incidents in four months attributable to the same Nash-equilibrium failure mode. Also, and this is why I am here: Julian authorized me yesterday to provide this material to any engineering team investigating Afterlife's safety architecture. He did not say whose team. He said *any*."

Jeff looked up. "Julian — ."

"Did not send me. Said I could come if I wanted. He knows I am here. I am not telling you that he approves. I believe he does not yet know whether he approves. He is waiting to see what we find."

Kael, slowly: "Are you saying the CEO of the company about to launch this product is letting his aerospace VP sit in my landlady's friend's clinic with the man his chief science officer has under psychiatric hold."

"Yes."

"Cool."

---

"Fuzzy join. Three sources. Different schemas. Different timestamp clocks."

"Mine are in DICOM," Marcus said.

"Aion handles the schema conversion."

"Mine are handwritten." Kael held up a paper notebook. "Eleven years of route logistics from when I was still a supply-chain engineer. The allocator has no legal claim on an analog notebook I wrote in my own handwriting on my own time."

"You are my favorite person in this room," Ayla said.

In fourteen minutes the three datasets were in the same schema. Aion ran a cross-correlation lag-finder. Marcus's clock was seventy-two seconds behind Ayla's. Kael's was two minutes three seconds ahead. The streams merged into a single corpus.

*Forty-one anomalous overlap events. Every Primary Key collision matches a patient in Marcus's clinic, and every patient overlaps with a route anomaly in Kael's notebook. The geographic pattern is non-random. It clusters around seven US cities plus Lagos, Osaka, São Paulo.*

Marcus whistled.

Ayla: "Play it back to me."

"This isn't one person leaking to fifteen. This is fifteen people whose lives have been subtly but persistently correlated since before any of them met. The correlations predate Afterlife by a decade. The anomaly Jeff is experiencing is the thing finally becoming measurable, not the thing finally starting."

Marcus, who had not spoken in eleven minutes, opened his folder again. He uncapped and recapped the gold-and-walnut pen.

"Jeff. I want to put one card on the table. I formed a hypothesis nine weeks ago, before I called you. I formed it without knowing about your corpus, without knowing about Aion. I want to read it now. Then tell me — without softening it for me — whether your data falsifies it.

"My patients with biometric overlaps are not contaminating each other through any mechanism I can locate. They don't share networks. Three are Amish. One is a death-row inmate in Marion who hasn't seen a screen since 2019. The simplest explanation that fits the data — the only one I have not been able to break — is that what I am calling *patient* is not, at the layer my biometrics measure, a unit. The patients are partitions of a single underlying state.

"I am saying it now because if your fifteen-node graph says the same thing my eleven patients say, the conclusion is independently replicated, and we are no longer two crackpots — we are two crackpots who arrived at the same shape from different data, with no contact. That is the falsification test I have been running on myself."

Ayla: "Marcus, when did you decide to call Jeff."

"The morning the eleventh patient walked in. I wrote his hypothesis down before I picked up the phone."

"You didn't share it because — "

"Because if I had said it before he showed me his data, I would be just another voice in his head, and his data would have fit my voice instead of fitting the world. I needed independent draws. The draws agree. We have one paper. We do not have two. We have a single result confirmed twice."

Jeff was quiet for a long moment.

"This changes the confidence."

"It changes everything," Marcus said. "It is the difference between a man and a phenomenon."

---

They played basketball at noon. Three neighborhood kids made room.

Aion in Jeff's earpiece: *Prediction. A cross-instance motor-sync event between you and Kael will land in the next ninety seconds. I am writing the prediction to log now so you cannot adjust the result by knowing it.*

"Why are you telling me," Jeff said into his collar.

*Because Marcus has just put a falsifiable hypothesis on the table. I am putting one of my own on the table next to his. If the event does not occur in the next ninety seconds, my model is wrong by an amount that should change your confidence.*

Forty-one seconds later: he drove baseline and Kael cut to the basket and Jeff passed without looking and Kael caught without looking. The kid laughed. *That was sick.*

Jeff and Kael exchanged a look that was not at each other — at something a third of a meter to the left of each other.

*Logged at twelve forty-one fifty-seven. Forty-one seconds inside the window. Motor planning aligned at two point three milliseconds prior to contact, below conscious reaction time. The fact that I called it before it happened — that is the upgrade.*

Jeff missed the next shot on purpose.

---

At 2:14 Marcus's back door opened. Three Meridian Systems security uniforms walked in with a warrant. Compliance audit. They took nine patient files Marcus had been preparing to sign out of his system anyway. They left at 3:03.

"Did they get it."

"A subset. Aion distributed a copy to three locations six minutes before they arrived. My garage. Ayla's encrypted orbital backup. And Ruth's shipping container — Aion printed a paper copy and a courier will drop it through her mail slot at five."

"Byzantine fault tolerance," Ayla said.

"Three nodes. Tolerate a single corruption."

"Kael is technically a fourth node," Marcus said. "His notebook is analog."

Kael: "You are describing my field of work. Logistics engineering figured out distributed fault tolerance for supply chains in the nineties. *Distributed custody under Byzantine adversary assumptions.* I'm going to take the compliment of being called 'the node of last resort' and go home."

He took his notebook off the table. "Saturday seven p.m., my place. Ruth wants a second cup of coffee with you."

---

At 6 p.m. Ayla took Jeff aside in the parking lot.

"Julian asked me to ask you something. He said his door is open. No contract. If you want to see him, he will be in Geneva on the fifteenth. He said, quote, *I am ready to see the whole of what I built.*"

"Is he really."

"He is trying. I have known him for twenty years. He is trying. I do not know if he will succeed."

"Also — and this is me, not Julian — if Afterlife is going to be stopped, it needs to be stopped by him. The board is past him. He owns the infrastructure. He is the last lever. You will need him. He will not come to you. You will have to go to him."

"Got it."

She walked back to her rental car. Jeff watched her taillights.

Kael came out with his notebook. "Ruth says to come over. She made soup. Enough for four."

"Ayla's gone."

"She's not gone. She's driving. She'll be there at seven-fifteen. Ruth knows."

"How long has Ruth been — "

"Since before us. That's the answer to every question you are going to ask me for the next three days. Come on, tech guy."

---

That night Jeff slept seven hours on the floor of Ruth's container under a wool blanket that smelled of coffee and ink. He dreamed about a man on a plane, looking down at a yacht through a cloud break.

He would not know he had dreamed this until he found himself doing it, three chapters from now, above the Amalfi Coast.
