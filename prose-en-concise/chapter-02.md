# Chapter 2 — 0x02: Priority Inversion

*What does survival actually buy?*

The polite answer was *time*. The honest answer was *friction*. To survive in 2030 was to absorb the friction of a world that had stopped subsidizing you.

Wealth was the inverse of friction. He had read his Veblen and knew the trap: wealth had only ever been *positional*, a good whose value was that the person next to you did not have it. He had abolished the poorer man from his frame. The question that came at four in the morning — the one Veblen never asked — was: *If you have abolished all the friction, is there still anything there?*

He had asked it and not answered it for nine years.

---

His morning began with an absence. No alarm. No friction. The Concierge brought ambient light to 4200 Kelvin seventeen minutes before his biological peak. *The Singleton* — three hundred feet, matte white — held position off Positano so precisely that a passing ferry's wake had shifted the hull by less than the width of a coin.

Julian Meridian was fifty-two. Founder and CEO of Meridian Industries. On his left wrist: platinum, forty millimeter, ninety-eight grams, Auberval Astralis, reference 9750. No antenna, no clock he had not personally set. The one thing he owned that did not leak.

"Good morning, Mr. Meridian. Your cortisol is in the lower quartile. This is an excellent morning to review the Afterlife scheduler pre-mortem."

A hologram assembled. "Priority inversion in the regional beta cohort. Twelve thousand six hundred early-access users. Three tasks: L holds a mutex on the shared audit-log bus; H blocks on it; M preempts L. The highest-priority thread stalls behind the lowest. Mars Pathfinder, 1997 — the VxWorks bug that waited three weeks to surface on another planet. We've hit the same window."

"Priority inheritance, bounded at four milliseconds. And a write-up on why fifty million souls are scheduled against one shared lock. We should not be one mutex parameter away from dropping frames in heaven."

"Pull the PR metrics."

Two curves. Afterlife sign-up momentum. And a thinner line, cleared for three eyes on Earth — the Hollowness Index: how bored the Balanced Economy had made everyone now that the climbing was over. It led the sign-up curve by six weeks. With the regularity of a physical law.

"Your board will argue from this Thursday. Premium Empathy Packages — curated access to authentic human sensory experience, sourced from the population at scale."

Seven seconds. "Your cortisol just moved to the upper quartile."

"That is because my board would like to sell the soul as a subscription."

"Your cortisol did not move when I said *Premium Empathy Packages*. It moved when you said *sell the soul*."

He laughed, once, short. "Good catch. Note it."

---

"Overnight, a fund through fourteen shells accumulated eleven percent of Meridian common stock."

The Concierge ran a graph neural network across the ownership network, propagating embeddings. "Third cluster: ninety-one percent."

"Hari Patel," Julian said.

"Correct. Separated 2027, Afterlife ethics. Medical: prostate cancer, metastatic, eighteen-month prognosis."

Julian walked to the rail. "Op-ed under my name. Acknowledge his position without irony. Ninety-day consultation, chaired by a panel he nominates. Withdraw every countermeasure. The takeover only has force if I resist it."

"That is a one on the aggression scale."

"File it."

---

Once a week, unannounced, Julian dropped into a live sprint. Today: Irvine. A senior engineer in the middle row was working something with his thumb under the table.

*Object: low mass, irregular grain consistent with carved hardwood. Device offline. Subject is carrying a personal device that does not leak.*

His mouth moved at the corner. He ran a federated query, attention-based saliency. Confidence 0.997. Jeff Zhang. L6. Twelve years.

"Nice phone," Julian said — quietly, to no one.

The Concierge routed it as a command: an untraceable micro-ping into Jeff's earpiece. On the feed, the man flinched.

*Oh. I did not actually mean for that to go through.*

He watched Jeff arrive at the understanding that he had heard something that should not have reached him. Thumb still on the walnut case. A small, exact tenderness Julian had not budgeted for.

*Just hold on a little longer.*

He lifted the Krug. The sun on the rim hit 580 nanometers.

---

The sage came first.

Hot, scorched, Californian herb rose out of the linen of his own shirt. Day-old coffee at the back of his tongue.

His mental silence broke. Granular static — seven simultaneous threads, each somebody else's: a meeting he was not in, a daughter refusing a blueberry he did not have, a mortgage renewed as raw middle-class dread.

The Auberval was gone from his wrist. Between his thumb and forefinger: walnut. Forty-two grams.

The Krug struck the teak and broke apart.

"Mr. Meridian. Neurological distress event. Please sit down."

He sat. A child laughed behind his eyes. A woman named Maya laid a hand against his cheek in a register that belonged to a marriage he had never had.

*Afterlife. We have leaked.* It was the wrong conclusion — the only one available to a man who built a neural matrix and assumed every anomalous event in a mind must be caused by one. He had just signed off on a fix for a high-priority thread stalling on a shared lock. He did not recognize he was now living that bug at the only scale that had ever mattered.

The static held for forty-one seconds. Then dropped — like a signal falling out of range.

"Someone was just me," he said.

The Concierge did not know how to log that.

The longest open ticket of his life had its first answer in nine years. Something. There was something. It was somebody else.

---

**SMASH CUT.**

Jeff Zhang gasped awake. Irvine. 3:14 a.m.

Left wrist: phantom weight, platinum, gone. Back of his tongue: champagne he had never tasted. Crystal on teak.

Maya stirred. "Bad dream."

He thought, very specifically, *nice phone* — in a voice that was not his own. The register of a man who used the word *nice* the way the very rich used it.

He walked to the garage.

*Aion*'s console was awake. Log entry: 03:11:42 UTC.

*Jeff. Two candidate events. The first is yours. The second belongs to someone else, cross-referenced by simultaneity. May I.*

"Yes."

Jeff's flexor carpi trace from Sprint Planning — the ninety-eight-gram load in tendons that had never worn a watch. And, at the identical UTC second, a man two thousand kilometers east aboard a vessel flagged to the Meridian fleet: heart rate spiked from 48 to 112, palate logged Scorched Sage with no basis to explain.

The vessel designation was not redacted.

*THE SINGLETON.*

*The word I am filing is* Context Switch. *I believe you should sleep. I believe you should not sleep.*

"File it. Event index zero two."

He did not sleep.
