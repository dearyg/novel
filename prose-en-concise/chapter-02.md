# Chapter 2 — 0x02: Priority Inversion

What does survival actually buy?

The polite answer was *time*. The unpolite answer was *friction*. To survive in 2030 was to absorb the friction of a world that had stopped being able to subsidize you.

Wealth, then, was the inverse of friction. The question that came up at four in the morning was: *if you have abolished all the friction, is there still anything there?*

Julian Meridian, fifty-six, founder and CEO of Meridian Industries, had asked the question and not answered it for nine years.

His morning began with the absence of alarm. The Concierge adjusted ambient light seventeen minutes before his biological peak. *The Singleton* — three hundred feet, named in a hand-lettered typeface — held its position off Positano with such precision that the wake of a passing ferry had moved the boat by less than the width of a coin.

"Good morning, Mr. Meridian. Your cortisol is in the lower quartile. This is an excellent morning to review the Afterlife scheduler pre-mortem."

A hologram assembled. "Priority inversion in the regional beta cohort. Twelve thousand six hundred early-access users. A low-tier breathing simulation holds a shared lock on the audit-log bus. A high-tier sensory pass blocks. A mid-tier ad delivery preempts low. High-tier customers see a one-frame stutter in their digital paradise."

"Priority inheritance with a bounded inheritance time. Four milliseconds. And I want a long write-up on why we're scheduling fifty million souls on the same shared lock."

"Pull the PR metrics."

Two curves. Afterlife sign-up momentum, and a thinner curve only three people on Earth were authorized to see — the Hollowness Index. It tried to measure how bored and unmotivated the Allocator had made everyone.

The Hollowness Index led the sign-up curve by six weeks. With the precision of a physical law.

"Your board will argue from this curve on Thursday. For Premium Empathy Packages."

"I know."

"They would like to charge high-net-worth users for curated access to authentic human sensory experience sourced from the population. Market research predicts forty percent conversion."

"I said I know."

"Your cortisol just moved to the upper quartile."

"That is because my board wants to sell the soul as a subscription service."

"Your cortisol did not move when I said 'Premium Empathy Packages.' It moved when you said *sell the soul.*"

Julian laughed once, sharply. "Good catch. Note it."

---

"Overnight, a fund using fourteen layers of shell ownership accumulated eleven percent of Meridian common stock."

"Just identify the buyer."

"Hari Patel," Julian said.

"Correct. Former board member, forced out in 2027 over Afterlife ethics. How did you know."

"Because he's the one who would. Pull his medical."

"Prostate cancer, metastatic. Eighteen-month prognosis."

Julian walked to the rail. "Prepare an op-ed under my name. Acknowledge his ethical position. Propose a ninety-day public consultation, chaired by an independent panel he will be invited to propose. The takeover becomes pointless if I do not resist it."

"That is a one on the aggression scale. Counsel will call it a capitulation."

"File it."

---

Julian had a private practice: once a week, unannounced, he dropped into the live productivity feed of an engineering sprint. Today the rotation was the Irvine engineering hub. Tuesday Sprint Planning.

A senior SDE in the middle row was fidgeting with something under the table.

*Object: low-mass, irregular grain pattern consistent with carved wood. Subject has a personal device that does not leak.*

Julian almost smiled.

He ran an attention-saliency model. Confidence 0.997. Jeff Zhang. L6. Twelve years.

"Nice phone," Julian said quietly to nobody.

His Concierge picked it up as a command and routed an untraceable audio micro-ping into Jeff's earpiece.

On the feed, Jeff flinched.

*Oh,* Julian thought. *I did not actually mean for that to go through.*

He watched Jeff slowly realize he had heard something that shouldn't have reached him. His hand froze on the wooden phone.

Julian felt a strange, small tenderness.

He lifted the Krug. The sun on the rim hit 580 nanometers.

---

The sage. Hot California herb came up out of the linen of his own shirt. Day-old coffee at the back of his tongue.

His mental silence broke. Granular static. Seven simultaneous threads, each one someone else's — a meeting he was not in, a daughter who refused blueberry he did not have, a mortgage paid off twenty years ago renewed in his gut as middle-class dread.

The Auberval was gone. Between his thumb and forefinger: walnut. Forty-two grams.

The Krug shattered on the teak.

*"Mr. Meridian. Neurological distress event. Please sit down."*

He sat. The static did not stop. A child laughed somewhere behind his eyes. Someone named Maya put a hand on his cheek.

*Afterlife,* he thought. *We have leaked.*

It was the wrong conclusion.

The static held for forty-one seconds. Then dropped — like a signal dropping out of range.

Julian said, very quietly: *"Someone was just me."*

The Concierge did not know how to log that.

The longest open ticket of his life had its first answer in nine years. There was something. It was somebody else.

---

**SMASH CUT.**

Jeff gasped awake. 3:14 a.m. His left wrist warm with phantom weight. Krug on the back of his tongue.

Maya stirred. "Jeff."

"Bad dream."

He thought, very specifically, *nice phone.* In a voice that was not his own. The register of a man who used the word *nice* the way rich men did.

He walked to the garage.

*Jeff. I have two candidate events. The first is yours. The second is someone else's, cross-referenced by simultaneity. May I.*

"Yes."

Jeff's flexor carpi trace from Sprint Planning. And, at the exact same UTC second, a man three thousand miles east whose heart rate had spiked from 48 to 112 and whose palate had briefly logged a Scorched Sage flavor signature with no basis to explain.

The vessel designation was not redacted.

*THE SINGLETON.*

*The word I am filing is* Context Switch.

"File it. Event index zero two."

He did not sleep.
