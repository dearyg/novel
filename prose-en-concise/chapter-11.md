# Chapter 11 — 0x0B: The Mirror Instance

The rain in {{user_location}} was the rain of a city that had decided to stay.

Jeff walked through the post-Culling shelter district near {{user_landmark}} with his jacket wet across the shoulders. Modular shelters. Communal water spigots. A double-wide trailer clinic. A hand-lettered sign reading YOU ARE NOT INVISIBLE. Mostly men, mostly between thirty and fifty, mostly with the broken profile of people who had been middle-class skilled professionals four years ago.

He felt every eye on him. He had come in his Irvine clothes. He was, in this neighborhood, a walking index of the class that had written the AI that had culled every man on this block.

He found the billboard. Currently cycling: LEAVE THE PAIN BEHIND. Under it, a man in three layers of donated jackets and a sleeping bag Jeff had ordered against his credit card two weeks earlier.

*◊ A fork. Walk toward him, or back away and keep the firewall up. ◊*

Jeff stood eight feet away and did not approach.

"You lost," the man said. He did not look up.

"No. I'm looking for someone who was hospitalized last week. Hypothermia."

"That was me. And I don't need a wellness check from Meridian or whoever sent you."

"Nobody sent me. I think we have something in common."

Kael laughed. Bitter. He stood up.

He was the same height as Jeff. Same build, under the emaciation. Same jawline. Same long, narrow hands. Jeff's facial-recognition model: cosine distance 0.11, bone-structure match 98.9%. Impossible for unrelated men.

"I think people like you build these systems and then when the systems eat people like me, you come here and look at us like we're data. You got the warm partition. I got the rain."

A long silence. Kael sat back down.

"Every guy on this block is sunset hardware. Every one of us was the last engineer maintaining some system that got auto-replaced. The allocator gives me a studio unit in Lacey if I apply. Basic medical. Food credits. The allocator is *fair*. Painfully, mathematically fair. I keep saying no to the studio because the studio is the same studio every other displaced engineer gets. The food credits are the same food credits. I go inside, I get a life identical to every other person the system threw out. At least on the street, I'm the one making the decisions."

He looked up at the billboard. "And the irony. The AI killed the economy where someone like me could claw my way up and matter. Now the same AI tells me I'm fine — I have enough — and you can't be rich anymore. And that asshole is selling the one thing left. He's selling *be special*. People are lining up. Not because they're dying. Because they're bored."

Jeff did not have a response.

---

"Tell me anyway."

"I felt you die."

Kael stopped. He looked up very slowly.

"When."

"Thursday night. Twenty-two forty-seven Pacific. I was on my kitchen floor in Irvine. Hypothermic. My skin was blue. For ninety seconds. Then the paramedics came — for you — and I warmed up on my kitchen floor."

Kael did not say anything for a long time.

"You're a tech guy."

"Yes."

"Aion. Explain what just happened to me, that I was felt by a tech guy from Irvine four states away."

Jeff held up his phone. The complete graph on fifteen nodes, rotating slowly.

"Fourteen others. Plus you. Plus me. Sixteen now. The graph is complete. Every pair correlated. The paramedics came to you because they were nearby, not because I called them."

Kael stared at the graph for a long time.

"Come with me."

---

Three blocks west. A vacant lot with a forty-foot shipping container. Matte green. Solar panels. A small copper antenna. A hand-painted *1480* over the door.

Kael rapped twice.

"Who."

"It's me, Ruth."

"Alone?"

"No. The tech guy."

"Oh, *that* tech guy. Fine. Shoes off."

The woman in the door was seventy-one. Two layered flannels. Silver hair cut by herself. Tortoiseshell glasses on a thin gold chain.

Inside: coffee, old newsprint. A copper-wire landline. A shortwave. A typewriter — Smith-Corona, 1974. An AM transmitter with its red on-air light dark.

"Ruth, Jeff. Tech guy. Jeff, Ruth. My landlady, sort of. Journalist. Before."

Ruth poured him coffee. "You're allocator-surveilled right now. They can't hear us in here. My container is lined with the same copper mesh the Elder uses."

"How do you know about the Elder."

"I used to cover him. He was CTO of IBM Global Services in 2011. He's my source."

"Will you tell me what the hell is happening."

Ruth set the thermos down. "I'll tell you what I won't tell you. I won't tell you that you're having a breakdown. I have watched seven friends get diagnosed with dissociation by company doctors since 2023 and I can tell the difference. You are not sick. You are right. Which is worse for your career than being sick would be. Get on the show next Thursday. Seven p.m."

"Your show."

"Fourteen-eighty AM. Three hours a week. I read the week's news. I take letters from listeners. Real letters. Paper. The last place where you can report an anomaly without it being algorithmically categorized and buried is a woman at a typewriter who the allocator has never indexed."

"You're off-grid."

"I'm off allocator. I pay taxes by mail. I have a paper driver's license. I am, as far as any recommendation engine is concerned, a ghost. I am the control group, Jeff. I am what your species looked like before the experiment."

"Why."

"Because I covered tech for thirty-four years. I watched everyone I respected ship products that broke things I loved. In 2022 I decided to conduct a personal experiment. I am eight years into it. I plan to die in it."

"Why are you telling me this."

"Because if you are about to fight your ex-employer in public, you will need places to sleep where the allocator cannot find you. This container will sleep three. We already have Kael. You are the third."

She poured him a second cup. On the wall, a paper calendar said April. A yellowing newspaper showed Julian's photograph from last Friday's press conference. Ruth had underlined: *Afterlife is not the destination. Afterlife is the bridge.*

In the margin, in red pen: *to what? asked the Ferris wheel, in a parking lot, in 2030.*

---

At dusk Jeff walked back. His phone came back online. Seven missed calls from Marcus.

"Tell me you're alive."

"I'm alive."

"Ruth's container is clean. Ayla Reyes flies in tomorrow. We meet at the clinic Saturday morning at nine."

"Okay."

"You understand you are not alone."

"Yes."

"Good. That's the whole game."

He texted Maya: *I'm okay. On my way home.*

She: *Come see the kids.*

Iris was awake when he came through the door at midnight. She had waited up.

"Daddy. The cold man is warm now."

"Yeah, bud. He is."

She nodded, satisfied. She went back to bed.

---

A Meridian surveillance vehicle photographed Jeff's arrival. Lena received the photograph at 12:41. She put it in a file and closed her laptop. She did not file it anywhere punitive. She had stopped collecting evidence for the hold. She was collecting evidence for something else. She did not yet know what.
