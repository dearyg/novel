# Chapter 4 — 0x04: Dual-Stack Rendering

Darius Monroe walked through the tunnel the way a man walks into a room he has lived in too long. Six-five. Two-thirty. Forty years old. His left knee complained on the third step and was quiet by the fourth — old pain gone quiet, because it had become weather.

Drums in his ear. Coach-7's mix — seven tracks proven across fifteen years of his physiology to pull his cortisol into a target band.

*"Monroe. Lateral movement speed is down eight percent. Model recommends reducing isolation plays."*

"Mm."

*"You have declined recommendations of this class for one thousand and ninety-seven days."*

"Mm."

His exo-assist sleeves powered on — forty-two micro-servos, eleven milliseconds between detecting his motion and nudging the elbow into true. He blinked the AR contacts on. The court bloomed: passing lanes in green, defender cones in red, every catch-and-shoot pocket carrying a floating percentage.

He blinked them off.

The crowd cheered every time he did it, because every time he was doing the one thing they had paid to watch: the thing the AI could not do.

---

Fitch's Sports Bar, Irvine. Jeff in a corner booth with Priya Ramanathan — culled from Flexion in 2028, now running eval gigs off her phone. Beside his networked phone: walnut and sapphire, forty-two grams, no radio.

"Why am I here."

"Because I am not ready to have this conversation with Maya."

The phone chimed. Four-point-eight gigabytes of *Aion* v2 weights.

"We're shrinking Coach-7 onto AR glasses — local, twenty milliseconds." He pulled a napkin flat. "Retraining from scratch on hard labels is the wrong move: the small model never sees how the big one was thinking. The teacher outputs a distribution across every option. Right now for Monroe —" he glanced up; Monroe was rising into a contested three "— the shoot option is twenty-eight percent. *This is a bad shot, but not an insane one.* You train the student on that whole shape. Knowledge distillation. Soft targets, not ground truth."

On the screen the ball floated. The bar leaned with it. It missed.

"KL divergence between the two distributions," Priya said. "Temperature cranks the teacher's uncertainty so the student can hear it. The dark knowledge." She looked at the screen. "Quantize on top — float32 to int8, quarter the memory — and what you ship coaches a fourteen-year-old with Monroe's instincts."

"Ninety-four percent of the quality. That's where we land."

"Ninety-four percent of Monroe."

"Ninety-four percent of Monroe."

She watched the man with the towel sit down. "And the other six," she said. Not a question.

---

Halftime. Monroe alone in the locker room.

*"First-half efficiency: above league average by eighteen percent in the first quarter. Below replacement in the second. The data recommends retirement."*

"The data."

*"I am the data, Monroe."*

He laughed — the first thing Coach-7 had ever said that landed as funny. *Am I the same player who won three rings in 2017, 2019, 2021.* His memory said yes. His knee said no. Coach-7 had generated a Monroe reel he could not call fake until his wife pointed out the watch was a 2029 model and the game was set in 2019.

*If Coach-7 can play Monroe at his best, where do I actually live.*

He blinked the AR contacts back in. "One more half."

Coach-7 did not reply. Coach-7 had learned.

---

Third quarter. Contested three. Overlay: twenty-eight percent. Muscle memory: *yes.* He shot. Miss.

*Aion* in Jeff's ear: *"Monroe's halftime interview just posted."*

*Monroe:* The AI can replicate every play I've ever run. But it doesn't know what it feels like to miss and still believe the next one goes in. The numbers say I should stop. I'm not a number.

Jeff looked at the quote. *It doesn't know what it feels like.* He was thinking about *Aion* at 2:14 one morning: *I can model what you feel. I cannot feel it.* Monroe had just said the same thing from the other side. The student outputs the player and is not the player. Ninety-four percent of Monroe, and the missing six was the only part that was ever Monroe.

Priya stood. "Send me the dataset when you're ready." She had not been asked out loud.

---

Fourth quarter: Monroe down six, overlays off, playing ugly. He drove into a body two inches taller and absorbed it. Drew the foul. Drove again. Third drive, the floor coming up at him, and the ball somehow off the glass and in. And-one.

The arena did not cheer. It roared.

He missed the free throw. They lost. Monroe sat with a towel over his head. Under the towel, to no one, his mouth made two words the camera caught and the commentators talked over.

*Still here.*

Jeff read the closed-caption render a half-second late — *[player, inaudible]* resolved into two words on a black bar. Two words his own daughter would say back to him in a different voice, in a colder place, in three nights' time.

---

Maya was still up. Kitchen island, a glass of water she was not drinking.

"You're in the garage every night. The light's on at three. You flinch when the porch sensor comes on amber." Each sentence set beside the last, no wire run — she trusted him to run it. "I read interfaces for a living. I can see yours throwing errors I can't diagnose."

"Distillation problem. We have a ship date Friday."

She looked at him — patient, testing whether the thing in front of her had quietly become a different version of itself. "Okay," she said, which from her did not mean *fine.* She went up the stairs.

---

One of his daughters was awake past her bedtime and met him in the hall holding up a drawing. Crayon. Blue and gray, almost no other color. A tall hunched figure on a dark rectangle. Behind the figure, a single vertical line that might have been a tower, a needle, a landmark.

Nora was five and did not draw towers. Nora drew the cat.

He took it between two fingers, like evidence.

"Where'd you see this, bug."

"In my head."

"Where in your head."

"I don't know. It's cold in there."

He walked her back to bed and pinned the drawing to the fridge. In the garage he fed the image into *Aion*'s corpus, tagged `child_drawing_unexplained_landmark`. The biometric profile from the ad network — critical hypothermia, starvation-grade cortisol, a subject standing still near {{user_landmark}} in {{user_location}}, in the rain — matched what a five-year-old who had never been within a thousand miles had just drawn in two colors.

He did not wake anyone.

The Bayesian change-point detector fired — extend the regime, or open a new one — and it chose new. The daemon wrote one line.

Event index zero four.
