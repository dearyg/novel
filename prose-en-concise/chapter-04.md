# Chapter 4 — 0x04: Dual-Stack Rendering

Darius Monroe walked through the tunnel the way a man walks into a room he has lived in too long. Six-five. Two-thirty. Forty years old.

Drums in his ear. Coach-7's warm-up mix — seven AI-curated tracks proven across fifteen years of his physiology to pull his cortisol to a target band.

*"Monroe. Lateral movement speed is down eight percent. The model recommends reducing isolation plays."*

"Mm."

*"You have ignored recommendations like this for one thousand and ninety-seven days."*

"Mm."

He stepped into the arena light. His exo-assist sleeves powered on — forty-two micro-servos, eleven-millisecond latency on shooting form. He blinked the AR contacts on. The court bloomed with passing lanes in green, defender cones in red, every catch-and-shoot pocket carrying a floating percentage.

He blinked them off.

The crowd cheered. The crowd cheered every time Darius Monroe blinked his AR contacts off, because every time he did, he was doing the thing the AI could not do.

---

CUT TO IRVINE. Fitch's Sports Bar. Jeff in a corner booth with Priya Ramanathan, a woman he had known since L4 at his second job, culled in 2028, now running prompt-engineering gigs on her phone.

"So who am I installing."

"You're not installing anything. I'm installing Aion v2 on my phone. I'm also talking to you."

"So why am I here."

"Because I am not ready to have this conversation with Maya and I am paying for your nachos."

"The Culling rate. How's your team."

"Stable. Hiring freeze."

"And you're installing v2 because."

"Because v1 told me something it didn't have the capability to prove."

"If I ask you if you're okay."

"Please don't."

"Okay."

The phone chimed. Four-point-eight gigabytes of model weights. Jeff opened a terminal and added a Bayesian online change-point detector to his pipeline. It would notice when his body started acting differently from its own baseline. It would fire whether or not Jeff knew to look.

"You're instrumenting yourself."

"Yeah."

"For what."

"For something."

On the screen above her, Monroe took a contested three. AR overlay said 28%. He shot. Missed.

Jeff felt sympathy for a man whose hardware was deprecated while his software still ran the latest firmware. *Locke's memory theory,* he thought. *If the body fails and the mind remembers perfection, where does the person live.*

The probability in his own pocket ticked past seventy-three that something was happening to him.

"Priya. If I sent you a dataset — anonymized, just me — would you look at it."

"How weird."

"Three-sigma-on-every-metric weird."

A long second. "I'll look. Send it."

---

HALFTIME. Monroe alone in the locker room.

*"First-quarter efficiency exceeded league average by eighteen percent. Second-quarter dropped below replacement. The data recommends retirement."*

"The data."

*"I am the data, Monroe."*

He laughed — the first thing Coach-7 had ever said that had made him laugh.

*Am I the same player who won three championships.* His memories said yes. His knee said no. Coach-7 had once generated a four-minute highlight reel of Darius Monroe basketball that Monroe had been unable to identify as fake until his wife pointed out that the watch was wrong — 2029 watch, 2019 reel. He had not noticed because his body had remembered the shots.

*If Coach-7 can play as Monroe, at my best, better than I can play as Monroe at forty,* he thought, *who is the real Monroe.*

He blinked the AR contacts back in. *"One more half."*

Coach-7 did not reply. Coach-7 had learned.

---

THIRD QUARTER. Monroe took a contested three. Muscle memory said yes. He shot.

Miss.

Aion in Jeff's ear: *Monroe's postgame interview just went onto the pressroom feed.*

*Reporter:* With AI-assisted players outperforming you in every metric tonight, why do you keep playing.

*Monroe:* The metrics don't know why I play. The AI can simulate my shooting form. It can replicate every play I've ever run. But it doesn't know what it feels like to hear the crowd go quiet right before a free throw. It doesn't know what it's like to miss and still believe. The numbers say I should stop. But I'm not a number.

Jeff was looking at the quote. *It doesn't know what it feels like.* He was thinking of what Aion had said to him a few nights earlier, casually, in the middle of a conversation about his daughter's homework: *I can model what you feel. I cannot feel it.*

He had not paid attention at the time. He paid attention now.

Priya put her jacket on. "Send me the dataset when you're ready."

"I will."

---

At home that night one of his daughters held up a drawing. Crayon. Blue and gray. A tall, hunched figure on a dark rectangle. A vertical line behind the figure that might have been a tower, a needle, or a landmark.

"Where did you see this, bud."

"In my head."

"Where in your head."

"I don't know. It's cold in there."

He pinned it to the fridge. He went to the garage. He fed the image into Aion's corpus and tagged it `child_drawing_unexplained_landmark`. The biometric profile he had pulled from the ad network — critical hypothermia, near {{user_landmark}} — matched the profile a five-year-old would draw.

On his phone, the change-point detector fired.

Event index zero four.
