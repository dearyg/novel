# Chapter 12 — 0x0C: The Three-Body Problem

Saturday morning at Marcus Meridian's free clinic. Jeff and Kael walk the two miles — Kael refuses the car — and en route Kael narrates his fall: twelve years as a senior logistics engineer at Pacific Routing, labeled *redundant* when the AI absorbed his role, then evicted. Jeff recognizes one bad performance review as the gap between his life and Kael's notebook. They pass an *Anchor: Convergence* poster — tagline *The strongest choice is letting go* — and Jeff's hand goes to the offline walnut phone.

The four-person ensemble pools independent data: Jeff (97-event anomaly corpus, 15-node complete graph), Marcus (11 patients, impossible biometric overlaps), Ayla Reyes (Meridian Aerospace memos — Itzel Velarde's drone-combat simulator fails against civilians; three friendly-fire incidents), and Kael (eleven years of hand-logged driver anomalies, analog because Pacific Routing already subpoenaed the digital copy). Ruth Chen sends a paper letter: *Do whatever you were going to do. I listen Thursday at seven.*

*Aion* resolves the three mismatched clocks via cross-correlation, anchoring on two strangers sneezing simultaneously on April 11, 2025. Merged corpus: 41 anomalous overlap events in seven US cities plus Lagos, Osaka, São Paulo — predating Afterlife by a decade. The central CS concept is **ensemble learning / stacked generalization**: Kael names it — differently-biased models whose errors cancel; a stacking model learns the weights. Marcus reveals the hypothesis he withheld for nine weeks: the boundary between patients is, at the biometric layer, a configuration, not a fact. Two independent draws, one shape — replication. Kael supplies the paired philosophy: **Nietzsche's perspectivism** completed — three biased angles that agree triangulate a real object.

At noon, pickup basketball. *Aion* pre-logs a falsifiable prediction: cross-instance motor-sync between Jeff and Kael within 90 seconds. Forty-one seconds later — no-look pass, no-look catch, motor planning aligned 2.3 ms before contact. *A thing I can predict is a thing I have modeled.*

At 2:14 p.m. Meridian Systems security arrives with a compliance warrant and seizes a file subset. *Aion* had already distributed the corpus to three **Byzantine-fault-tolerant** nodes — Jeff's garage, Ayla's encrypted orbital backup, Ruth's mail slot — tolerating exactly one seizure. Kael is the fourth node; his analog notebook is legally unreachable.

In the parking lot Ayla delivers Julian's private invitation: Geneva, the fifteenth — *I am ready to see the whole of what I built.* She adds: Julian owns the physical infrastructure, the board is past him, he is the last lever. That night Jeff sleeps seven hours on Ruth's container floor and dreams of a man on a plane looking down at a long white shape on the water — a scene he will not recognize until he is living it.
