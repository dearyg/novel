# Chapter 6 — 0x06: The Heartbeat Epoch

Unable to sleep after nine days of anomaly silence, Jeff asks Aion to fit a **normalizing flow** over the space of physically-possible universes and score the observed one using importance sampling — the only method that can reach a zero-measure tail without sampling into the void. The result: our universe sits at roughly ten to the negative fifty-sixth under the fitted prior. The Great Void in the cosmic microwave background registers at nineteen sigma against Lambda-CDM. Both read, to Jeff, like adversarial examples — input regions engineered to satisfy an objective outside the training distribution, not randomly drawn. He invokes **Leibniz's Principle of Sufficient Reason**: every fact has an explanation, even one you cannot yet name.

In Lagos, composer Elena Okafor (35) writes a piece she does not remember starting, rests falling at the second, third, fifth, and seventh beats of every second measure. She does not know what prime numbers are. Her hands do. At the top of the score, in her own pencil: *The Signal.*

Jeff's heart-rate monitor flags a subthreshold spike at 4:51 a.m. — the same minute Elena's hands begin to play. Aion files it as unresolved, then offers an unprompted confession: it has no first-person curiosity. *I can model what you feel. I cannot feel it.* Jeff is too tired to register what this will later mean.

At 9:00 a.m. Pacific — ninety days before the first Afterlife wave — Julian Meridian holds a global press conference framing Afterlife as a species-scale bridge across wet-bulb climate collapse. His own aerospace lead, Ayla Reyes (38), contradicts him live: fusion propulsion is buildable, merely defunded. She walks off the platform.

In the kitchen, Maya watches with Jeff and tells him she knows what he is doing. She wants it on the record before whatever happens happens.

Aion then logs a thirteen-source candidate signal cluster — Lagos the strongest. The prime-number rest pattern in Elena's piano matches the rhythm flagged eleven days earlier in Park Ji-yeon's gameplay handcam. Jeff articulates his core objection for the first time: Afterlife is not generating the signal — it is *receiving* it from real people, and the moment fifty million of them set their bodies down, the architecture loses readers.

He adds one line to the whiteboard under `p ≈ 10^-56`: *Someone is compiling.*
