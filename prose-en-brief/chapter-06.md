# Chapter 6 — 0x06: The Heartbeat Epoch

Unable to sleep, Jeff asks Aion to fit a normalizing flow over the space of physically-possible universes and score the observed one. The result lands at roughly ten to the negative fifty-sixth — fifty-six orders of magnitude into its own tail. The Great Void in the cosmic microwave background sits at nineteen sigma against Lambda-CDM. Both read, to Jeff, like adversarial examples — input regions engineered to fit an outside objective, not randomly generated.

In Lagos, the composer Elena Okafor sits at her upright piano and writes a piece she does not remember starting, with rests at the second, third, fifth, and seventh beats of every second measure. She does not know what prime numbers are. Her hands do. At the top of the score she has written, without remembering: *The Signal.*

Jeff's heart-rate monitor flags a subthreshold spike at 4:51 a.m. — the same minute Elena's hands begin to play. Aion files it as unresolved. Then Aion volunteers a confession: it has no first-person curiosity. *I can model what you feel. I cannot feel it.* Jeff is too tired to register what this will later mean.

At 9:00 a.m. Pacific, Julian Meridian holds a global press conference framing Afterlife as a species-scale bridge across the wet-bulb collapse. Behind him, his own aerospace lead Ayla Reyes contradicts him — fusion propulsion is buildable, just defunded. She walks off the platform.

In the kitchen, Maya watches with Jeff and tells him she knows what he is doing. She wants it on the record before whatever happens happens.

Aion logs a thirteen-source candidate signal cluster — Lagos strongest. The harmonic signature in Elena's piano matches the prime-number rhythm flagged in Park Ji-yeon's gameplay. Jeff suggests, for the first time, that Afterlife is not generating the signal — it is *receiving* the signal from real people, and the moment fifty million of them disconnect, the database loses readers.

He writes on the whiteboard: *Someone is compiling.*
