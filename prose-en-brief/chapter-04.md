# Chapter 4 — 0x04: Dual-Stack Rendering

Darius Monroe, forty, plays the second half of his career against AI-augmented rookies. His exo-assist sleeves run a closed loop on his shooting form; his AR contacts overlay every passing lane and shot probability. Each time he disables them, the crowd cheers.

In a sports bar in Irvine, Jeff meets Priya Ramanathan — a former colleague culled in 2028, now running prompt-engineering gigs. While they talk, Jeff installs Aion v2 on his phone and deploys a Bayesian online change-point detector against his own biometrics. It will fire whether or not he knows to look.

He asks Priya to review an anonymized dataset of his anomalies. She agrees.

On the screen above them, Monroe takes a contested three. His muscle memory disagrees with the AI's probability estimate. He shoots. He misses. Jeff feels sympathy for a man whose hardware is deprecated while his software still runs the latest firmware.

In his postgame interview, Monroe says the AI can replicate every play he has ever run but does not know what it feels like to miss and still believe. Jeff remembers what Aion told him: *I can model what you feel. I cannot feel it.*

That night one of his daughters hands him a crayon drawing — a tall hunched figure on a dark rectangle, a vertical line behind him. *In my head*, she says. *It's cold in there.* The figure matches the location of the homeless man whose biometrics had merged with his.

Jeff pins the drawing to the fridge. The change-point detector fires. Event index zero four.
