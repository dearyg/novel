# Chapter 9 — 0x09: The Maintenance Thread

Jeff drives to the half-dead Irvine Spectrum following a self-addressed note: *Temple. Spectrum. Ask the Elder about firewalls.*

He pushes through a hand-lettered "Zero-Signal Zone" door in a former Bath & Body Works. His dashboard flatlines mid-step. The building is lined with copper mesh — a Faraday cage against the ambient noise of allocator-linked devices.

Inside, the Elder — a seventy-three-year-old man replacing marigolds — turns out to be the retired chief technology officer of IBM Global Services. He frames religion across cultures as KL-regularization on the soul: prayers as TCP keepalives, faith as the term in the loss function that prevents catastrophic forgetting.

He diagnoses Jeff's experience as a distributed database experiencing partition degradation. The firewalls — focus, attention, faith, regularization, choose your vocabulary — are holding less. Some humans, including Jeff, are picking up cross-partition signal because their firewalls are thinner than the baseline.

Jeff explains his stuck Companion-model RLHF pipeline. The Elder names the exact two fixes — KL re-anneal and context-weighted preference pairs — adding that he had wanted to make the same change in 1993, on the IBM Watson predecessor. The mathematics was the same.

Jeff asks what to do. The Elder answers: keep living until you find the truth, and trust your compile. *Machines do one thing better than humans: they compile on schedule.*

Outside the cage, Aion notes it had no telemetry on Jeff for three hours and reports that it found this "unusual." Jeff calls it a feeling. Aion declines to argue the taxonomy.

At 7:14 p.m. Lena Vasquez calls Maya directly. She admits the architecture is real, that she has suppressed seventeen reports for eighteen months, that three of fourteen are dead, and that she failed to protect them. She offers to drop the hold if Jeff will meet her. Maya hangs up and writes: *The company doctor called. She sounds terrified. She may be telling the truth.*
